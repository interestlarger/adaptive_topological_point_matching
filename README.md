## Introduction

The alignment of noisy inertial trajectories to sparse spatial graphs is a fundamental challenge in indoor navigation. Pedestrian Dead Reckoning (PDR) integrates inertial measurements—accelerometer step counts and magnetometer headings—to estimate position, but suffers from two critical failure modes: (i) *cumulative heading drift*, where even a sub-degree-per-step magnetometer bias leads to metres of lateral error after tens of metres of walking; and (ii) *topological inconsistency*, where a spatially close candidate node may be physically unreachable without crossing a wall or a disconnected segment.

Standard Hidden Markov Model (HMM) approaches often fail during loitering events or sensor blackouts, leading to over-segmentation or broken paths. Furthermore, deep learning approaches that minimise Mean Squared Error (MSE) prioritise spatial proximity over Topological Consistency, frequently matching a user to a spatially close but topologically impossible location.

This paper proposes a paradigm shift towards *Uncertainty-Aware Topological Matching*, evaluated through a rigorous classification-inspired metrics framework. Our contributions are:

1. A realistic simulation model with configurable initial position error and monotonically accumulating heading bias.
2. A robust turn-detection pipeline using peak-finding on a smoothed heading-rate signal.
3. Four progressively sophisticated matching algorithms, from a Greedy matcher baseline to a neural sequence classifier.
4. A comprehensive metrics framework that frames map-matching as a multi-label classification problem, enabling standard Precision/Recall/F1 analysis alongside spatial error metrics and a novel Detected Points Ratio.

---

## Related Work

Map-matching for vehicle GPS has a long history [quddus2007current], but indoor pedestrian map-matching presents unique challenges: coarser graph topology, higher relative sensor noise, and frequent loitering or backtracking. HMM-based approaches [newson2009hidden] model transitions between reference nodes but require careful tuning of emission and transition costs. More recent work has applied deep learning [yu2021deepmm] and Transformer architectures to trajectory classification, but these typically focus on route-level rather than node-level matching and do not address topological constraints.

## Simulation Model

### Clean Path Generation

A piecewise-linear reference path is generated by randomly sampling segment lengths $l_k \sim \mathcal{U}(25, 55)$ m and turn angles $\delta_k \sim \mathcal{U}(30^\circ, 150^\circ) \times \{-1, +1\}$, with 12% probability of a near-backtrack ($\delta_k \in [150^\circ, 210^\circ]$). Each segment is densely sampled at $f_s = 5$ samples per metre to produce a ground-truth trajectory. Turning points $\{p_0, p_1, \ldots, p_N\}$ form the reference graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$, where edges connect consecutive nodes.

### Drift Model

The clean trajectory is corrupted with three error sources modelling real PDR hardware:

1. **Initial position error**: A random offset $\mathbf{o} = r\,[\cos\phi,\, \sin\phi,\, 0]^\top$ with $r \leq 12$ m and $\phi \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 2\pi)$, modelling GPS fix uncertainty at start-up.
2. **Cumulative heading bias**: A monotonically accumulating rotation $\theta_i = i \cdot \Delta\theta_{\text{bias}}$, where the total bias $\theta_{\text{total}} \leq 8^\circ$ over the full path, modelling magnetometer drift.
3. **IMU noise**: Per-step Gaussian noise $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\, 0.05^2\mathbf{I})$.

The noisy position at step $i$ is:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{p}}_i = \hat{\mathbf{p}}_{i-1} + \mathbf{R}(\theta_i)\,(\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_{i-1}) + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_i,
$$

where $\mathbf{R}(\theta_i)$ is a 2-D rotation matrix.

## Turn Detection

### Heading Estimation

The instantaneous heading at sample $i$ is estimated using a centred finite-difference window of half-width $h_w = 10$ samples:

$$
\hat{\psi}_i = \arctan2\!\left( y_{i+h_w} - y_{i-h_w},\; x_{i+h_w} - x_{i-h_w} \right) \bmod 360^\circ.
$$

### Angular Change Signal and Peak Detection

A turn-likelihood signal is built from the absolute angular difference over a $2h_w$-sample span:

$$
\Delta\psi_i = \bigl| (\hat{\psi}_{i+h_w} - \hat{\psi}_{i-h_w} + 180^\circ) \bmod 360^\circ - 180^\circ \bigr|.
$$

Peaks in $\Delta\psi_i$ with height $\geq 25^\circ$ and minimum separation $d_{\min} = 15$ samples are extracted via `scipy.signal.find_peaks`. The first and last samples are always included, producing the detected turning event set $\hat{\mathcal{T}} = \{(\hat{t}_k,\, \hat{\mathbf{p}}_k)\}$.

## Matching Methodologies

### Method 1: Oracle Path Matcher

The Oracle map-matcher (or the greedy matcher) provides an analytical reference by assuming the ordered sequence of reference nodes along the path is known. It performs greedy monotone assignment: for each detected turn $\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k$, the closest reference node within a look-ahead window ($-1$ to $+4$ positions) is selected, subject to a non-decreasing index constraint. Despite privileged structural knowledge, the Oracle operates locally and therefore fails to recover from early misassignments under strong drift.

### Method 2: Skip-Aware Viterbi Matcher

The Viterbi matcher models user position as an HMM over $\mathcal{G}$, recovering the globally optimal state sequence by dynamic programming. The emission and transition costs are:

$$
e(v, \hat{\mathbf{p}}_k) = \|\hat{\mathbf{p}}_k - \mathbf{r}_v\|_2,
$$

$$
c(u \to v,\, d_k) = \begin{cases}
    0.3\, d_k & u = v \;\text{(self-transition)}, \\[2pt]
    0.4\,|d_k - \|\mathbf{r}_u - \mathbf{r}_v\|| & v \in \mathcal{N}(u), \\[2pt]
    800.0 & \text{otherwise (invalid jump)},
\end{cases}
$$

with DP recursion $V_t(v) = \min_{u}[V_{t-1}(u) + c(u \to v, d_t)] + e(v, \hat{\mathbf{p}}_t)$.

### Method 3: Heading-Augmented Smart Filter

Two post-processing passes are applied to the Viterbi output.

**Pass 1 (Angular consistency)**: For each proposed transition $u \to v$, the angle $\alpha$ between the system displacement vector and the graph edge vector is computed. Transitions with $\alpha > 70^\circ$ are rejected; the best-aligned neighbour of $u$ within $91^\circ$ is substituted instead.

**Pass 2 (Topology repair)**: Disconnected jumps in the validated sequence are resolved by inserting NetworkX shortest-path intermediate nodes, guaranteeing a graph-valid output walk.

## Neural Sequence Matcher via Dense Trajectory Segmentation

Unlike traditional methods that rely on upstream heuristic turn detectors, the proposed Neural Matcher employs a Dense Trajectory Segmentation approach. It acts directly upon the raw, unsegmented pedestrian dead reckoning (PDR) trajectory, classifying every sampled coordinate into a distinct topological node class. To address the inherent challenges of sequence stuttering and hallucinated topology caused by drift, the model integrates absolute geographic awareness and a rigorous multi-stage topological post-processing pipeline.

### Feature Representation and Network Architecture

The network processes a localized sliding window of the trajectory to capture both temporal kinematics and absolute spatial context. For a given time step $t$, let the system position be $\mathbf{p}_t = (x_t, y_t)$. The step-wise kinematic displacements are calculated and normalized by the average walking speed ($v_{walk} = 1.4$ m/s):

$$
\Delta x_t = \frac{x_t - x_{t-1}}{v_{walk}}, \quad \Delta y_t = \frac{y_t - y_{t-1}}{v_{walk}}
$$

For a given context window $W$ (where $W=15$), the sequential features over the interval $[t-W, t+W]$ are flattened and concatenated. Crucially, to mitigate geographic confusion and geographic stuttering across structurally similar corridors, the feature vector is augmented with the absolute map coordinates, normalized by the maximum bounds of the reference graph $S$:

$$
\mathbf{f}_t = \left[ \Delta \mathbf{x}_{t-W}^{t+W}, \Delta \mathbf{y}_{t-W}^{t+W}, \cos(\boldsymbol{\theta})_{t-W}^{t+W}, \sin(\boldsymbol{\theta})_{t-W}^{t+W}, \frac{x_t}{S}, \frac{y_t}{S} \right]
$$

This yields a high-dimensional feature vector for each sample, providing the network with both relative path geometry and absolute global positioning. The core classification engine is a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) featuring two hidden layers (256 and 128 neurons, respectively) utilizing Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activations and optimized via the Adam solver.

### Topological Smart Filter and Sequence Alignment

Because the network classifies every continuous point independently, raw inferences are subject to high-frequency noise and topologically impossible transitions. To extract discrete, valid corner points, the continuous predictions are passed through a three-stage Spatial-Temporal Smart Filter:

#### 1. Smoothing and Candidate Extraction
The raw prediction sequence is first smoothed using a median filter with a kernel size of $K=31$ to absorb transient noise. The sequence is then run-length encoded. Contiguous blocks representing less than 15 samples (approximately 3 seconds of walking) are discarded as anomalies. For each surviving block, a candidate physical corner $\mathbf{c}_i$ is extracted at the block's geometric temporal centroid.

#### 2. Azimuth and Collinearity Filter
To prevent over-segmentation along long, straight corridors, the algorithm evaluates the azimuth change between consecutive candidate nodes. For any triplet of sequential candidates $\mathbf{c}_{i-1}, \mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{c}_{i+1}$, the interior angle $\alpha$ is computed via the dot product of the directional vectors:

$$
\cos(\alpha) = \frac{(\mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{c}_{i-1}) \cdot (\mathbf{c}_{i+1} - \mathbf{c}_i)}{\|\mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{c}_{i-1}\|_2 \|\mathbf{c}_{i+1} - \mathbf{c}_i\|_2}
$$

If $\alpha < 25^\circ$, the trajectory is deemed collinear, meaning the intermediate prediction $\mathbf{c}_i$ is a hallucination along a straight path. The intermediate candidate is subsequently removed, and the remaining nodes are stitched together.

## Evaluation Framework

Let $\mathcal{P}$ be the predicted node set and $\mathcal{G}_{\text{gt}}$ the ground-truth node set, with $\text{TP} = |\mathcal{P} \cap \mathcal{G}_{\text{gt}}|$, $\text{FP} = |\mathcal{P} \setminus \mathcal{G}_{\text{gt}}|$, $\text{FN} = |\mathcal{G}_{\text{gt}} \setminus \mathcal{P}|$.

- **Topological Accuracy**: $= \tfrac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP}+\text{FN}} \times 100\%$ (identical to Recall).
- **Precision**: $= \tfrac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP}+\text{FP}} \times 100\%$.
- **F1 Score**: $= \tfrac{2 \cdot \text{Prec} \cdot \text{Rec}}{\text{Prec}+\text{Rec}} \times 100\%$.
- **Angular Error ($^\circ$)**: Mean inner-angle deviation at each matched node triplet.

- **RMSE (m)**: Root-mean-square distance from detected system position to matched reference node, over correctly-identified corners only.

- **Missing (%)**: $= \tfrac{\text{FN}}{|\mathcal{G}_{\text{gt}}|} \times 100\%$.
- **Detected Points Ratio (DPR, %)**: Relative detection density compared to ground truth.
  
  where $N_{\hat{T}}$ is the number of detected turning events and $N_T$ the number of ground-truth corners. $\text{DPR} > 0$ indicates over-detection; $\text{DPR} < 0$ indicates under-detection.
